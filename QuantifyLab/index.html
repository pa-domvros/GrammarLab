Distribution Determiners

Each

Followed by: noun
Verb Number: singular
Example: Each day passes quietly.

Followed by: of + noun/pronoun
Verb Number: singular or plural
Example: Each of them has/have been given a single room.

Every

Followed by: (adjective) + noun
Verb Number: singular
Example: Every new student is given a placement test.

Followed by: one + of + noun/pronoun
Verb Number: singular
Example: Every one of us is given a test.

Either

Followed by: noun
Verb Number: singular
Example: Either answer is possible.

Followed by: of + noun/pronoun
Verb Number: singular/plural
Example: Either of the two answers is/are possible.

All

Followed by: noun, of + pronoun/noun
Verb Number: plural
Example: All students/of us were given a test.

Both

Followed by: noun, of + pronoun/noun
Verb Number: plural
Example: Both of these answers are possible. / The answers are both possible.

None

Followed by: of + pronoun/noun
Verb Number: singular
Example: None of them is going to come.

Neither...nor / Either...or

Rule: Two singular subjects
Verb Number: singular/plural
Example: Neither Mary nor John have/has any problems. / Either Mary or John can do it.

Rule: One singular and one plural subject
Verb Number: plural (agrees with the subject closest to the verb)
Example: Neither the teacher nor the students are in favour of this exam.

Rule: When "you" is the second subject
Verb Number: plural
Example: Neither she nor you have anything to say.

Rule: When "you" is the first subject
Verb Number: plural/singular
Example: Neither you nor she have/has anything to say.

Rule: Two subjects, neither is a person
Verb Number: agrees with the last subject
Example: Neither your manners nor your social position is acceptable. / Neither your social position nor your manners are acceptable.

Some, Any and Compounds

Points to Remember

  • Some and any are used before countable or uncountable nouns.
  • Some and its compounds (somebody, somewhere, etc.) are used in affirmative sentences, when we make an offer or when we expect a positive answer.
  • Any and its compounds are used in interrogative or negative sentences.
  • No and its compounds can replace any when the negation is in the noun.
    Example: I haven’t got any time. = I have no time.

A Lot Of, Lots Of, Many, Much, Few, A Few, Little, A Little

Points to Remember

  • Many, few and a few + countable nouns.
  • Much, little and a little + uncountable nouns.
  • A lot of and lots of + countable/uncountable nouns.
  • Many, much usually in negative and interrogative sentences.
  • A little, a few = not much/many but enough, only a little.
  • Little, few = not much/many, almost none.
