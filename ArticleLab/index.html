The Definite Article: "The"

People

Don't Use "The"

For individual people or titles: Sir Alex Ferguson, Doctor Who.

Use "The"

For family names referring to the whole family: The Smiths have bought a new house.

Geographical Names

Don't Use "The"

For most cities, countries, continents, single mountains, single islands, and lakes: London, France, Asia, Mount Everest, Crete, Lake Victoria.

Exceptions: the Hague, the Vatican.

Use "The"

For countries with plural names or that show a form of government: The Netherlands, The United Kingdom.

For groups of islands, mountain ranges, rivers, seas, oceans, deserts, canals, gulfs, waterfalls: The Canaries, The Alps, The Amazon, The Sahara.

Places & Buildings

Don't Use "The"

For streets, parks, most bridges, and many famous buildings like stations, universities, cathedrals, castles, airports: Oxford Street, Hyde Park, Waterloo Station, Cambridge University.

Use "The"

For hotels, cinemas, theatres, galleries, museums, pubs, ships, and unique buildings: The Hilton, The Globe Theatre, The Titanic, The White House.

For names formed by "noun + of + noun": The Houses of Parliament.

Abstract & General Nouns

Don't Use "The"

For uncountable, abstract, or plural nouns in general statements: Life is beautiful. Honesty is a virtue. Oranges are sweet.

Use "The"

When referring to a specific instance of these nouns: The life of Winston Churchill. The honesty of this man. The oranges I bought yesterday.

Other Categories

Don't Use "The"

  • Games, meals, colours: chess, breakfast, blue.
  • Languages, magazines, companies, planets, most illnesses: English, Vogue, Microsoft, Mars, cancer.
  • Words like mum, dad, work, Heaven, nature.

Use "The"

  • For specific meals/games: The breakfast they serve is awful.
  • For nationalities: The English are reserved.
  • For English newspapers: The Times.
  • For musical instruments and inventions: play the piano.
  • For unique objects: the Sun, the Moon.
  • Before adjectives for a plural meaning: the poor, the rich.
  • Optional for: (the) flu, (the) mumps, (the) measles.

Watch Out!

"The" is not used before words like hospital, prison, church, school when talking about their intended purpose.

  • She goes to school. (She is a student)
  • I went to the school to see my son’s teacher. (A specific visit, not for the main purpose of being a student)

The Indefinite Article: "A / An"

When to Use "A / An"

  • Before a profession: She is a teacher.
  • Meaning "per": It costs 80p a pound.
  • Before Mr/Mrs/Miss/Ms + surname when the person is unknown: A Ms Lovelace wants to see you.
  • With a singular noun to represent a whole group: A dog can be a valuable friend.
  • Before drinks in spoken English when ordering: I’d like a black coffee, please.

The indefinite article is not used before uncountable or plural nouns.

Watch Out! Common Expressions

  • have a fever/temperature/cold/headache/toothache
  • catch a cold
  • make a recovery
  • What a pity / shame / a nuisance!

Watch Out! Reflexive vs. Reciprocal

  • Sue and Mary bought themselves new dresses. (Each bought a dress for herself).
  • Sue and Mary bought each other / one another new dresses. (Sue bought a dress for Mary, and Mary bought one for Sue).

The Possessive

Using Apostrophe ('s) or (')

  • Singular nouns (people/animals): the boy’s hair, the lion’s body.
  • Irregular plurals not ending in "s": the men’s room.
  • Compound nouns: his father-in-law’s car.
  • After two or more names (shared possession): John and Mary’s car.
  • Places: at the greengrocer’s.
  • Time expressions: last month’s programme.
  • Regular plurals ending in "s" (apostrophe only): the boys’ ball.

Using "of"

  • For objects and ideas: the leg of the chair, the suspense of the film.
  • To show one of many: A book of mine, One of John’s books.
  • For people in longer phrases: The mother of one of my closest friends.

Watch Out! Compound Adjectives

When a "number + noun" phrase acts as an adjective, it is singular and hyphenated.

  • A walk of three minutes → a three-minute walk.
  • A lesson of two hours → a two-hour lesson.
