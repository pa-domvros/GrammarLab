The Gerund

The gerund is a verb form which is used in a sentence as a noun. This means it can be used in several ways.

As a Subject

Sailing is fun.

As an Object

I love sailing.

After a Preposition

He is keen on sailing.

Subject of the Gerund

A subject for the gerund is needed when it's different from the subject of the main verb. When a subject is required, we use a possessive form, though an object form is common in informal speech.

He insisted on helping. (No subject needed, as "He" is the subject for both the verb and gerund)

He insisted on my helping. (Subject needed, because "I" will help, not "He")

Will you excuse my leaving early? (Formal)

Will you excuse me leaving early? (Informal, spoken English)

Tense Forms of the Gerund

The gerund has a present form and a perfect form (`having` + past participle). The perfect gerund shows the action happened in the past, but the present gerund is often also correct.

I regret having failed my test. (Correct)

I regret failing my test. (Correct)

Having lived in the city is expensive. (Wrong)

Having lived in the city made me happy. (Correct)

The Infinitive

The infinitive is a verb form that can follow verbs or adjectives.

Following a Verb

I want to stay a little longer.

Following an Adjective

I'm happy to see you here.

Types of Infinitive

A key distinction is between the infinitive with "to" and the infinitive without "to" (bare infinitive). Verbs like modals, `let`, `make` (in active voice), and `help` take the bare infinitive.

  • He let me go.
  • They made me do it. (But in passive: I was made to do it)
  • Can you help me do it? (Also correct: Can you help me to do it?)

Infinitive Forms

Negative Infinitive

Form: `not` + `(to)` infinitive.

He told me not to go out.

Progressive Infinitive

Form: `to` + `be` + `-ing`, used to emphasize an action in progress.

You seem to be doing well lately.

Perfect Infinitive

Form: `to` + `have` + `past participle`, used when the action has already happened.

He is said to have been a millionaire.

Watch Out! Perfect Infinitive with Modals

The perfect infinitive is the only way to show that the action of some modal verbs happened in the past.

He must be in. (Present)

He can't have been in. (Past)

Passive Forms

A look at how active forms change into the passive voice.

Active

I enjoy looking after my younger sister.

Passive

My younger sister enjoys being looked after by me.

Active

I need to clean this suit.

Passive

This suit needs to be cleaned.

Active (Perfect Infinitive)

Galileo is supposed to have invented the telescope.

Passive (Perfect Infinitive)

The telescope is supposed to have been invented by Galileo.

Gerund or Infinitive?

A guide to which verbs and expressions are followed by a gerund or an infinitive.

TYPE A: Verb + infinitive

He attempted to break the world record.

afford, agree, arrange, attempt, decide, fail, happen, hesitate, hope, learn, manage, neglect, offer, plan, prepare, pretend, promise, refuse, seem, swear, undertake, used to.

TYPE B: Verb + noun/pronoun + infinitive

She encouraged me to take the exam.

advise, allow, compel, encourage, forbid, force, instruct, oblige, order, permit, persuade, remind, request, teach, tell, warn, urge.

TYPE A or B

We expected to be there by midnight.

They expected us to be there by midnight.

ask, dare, expect, help, intend, like, love, want, wish,

TYPE C: Verb + question word + infinitive

I don’t know how to do it.

ask, decide, discover, explain, find out, forget, know, learn, remember, understand, wonder.

TYPE D: Verb + bare infinitive (without “to”)

You had better leave.

had better, let, make (active voice), would rather

TYPE A: Verb + gerund

He admitted stealing the money.

admit, avoid, can’t help, deny, enjoy, fancy, give up, go on, involve, keep (on), postpone, put off, spend (time), there’s no point (in), waste (time).

TYPE B: Verb + noun/pronoun + gerund

She likes people giving her presents. / She likes receiving presents.

adore, appreciate, can’t bear, detest, dislike, hate, imagine, it’s no good, it’s no use, it’s not worth, loathe (=hate very much), love, (don’t) mind, miss, prevent, risk, stop, suggest.

TYPE C: Verb + gerund or “that…..”

I regret telling/having told him the truth. / I regret (that) I told him the truth.

admit, deny, propose, recommend, regret, suggest,

TYPE D: Verb + to + gerund

I’m used to getting up early.

be/get used to, be/get accustomed to, look forward to, object to, take to (begin as a habit)

Expressions followed by the gerund

  • What about…?
  • How about …….?
  • I feel like …………
  • There’s no chance of……….
  • There’s no hope of ………….
  • What’s the point of………
  • There’s no point (in) ………
  • It’s no good……….
  • It’s no use…………
  • It’s not worth…………
begin, can’t bear, continue, hate, prefer, like, love, start

These have no significant change of meaning.

I love swimming/to swim in the swimming pool.

BUT: When these verbs follow "would", the infinitive is used.
I would love to swim in your swimming pool.

AVOID: We avoid following an -ing form one after the other.
I’m beginning realising that he’s lying. → I’m beginning to realise that he’s lying.

forget / remember / regret
  • + gerund = they refer to past events
    I remember seeing this film. (some time in the past)
  • + infinitive = they refer to events which take place after the action described by the verb.
    I remembered to post the letter. (first I remembered then I posted)
stop / go on
  • + gerund = doing sth
    He stopped trying. (He didn’t try any more)
    He went on speaking. (He continued his speech)
  • + infinitive = to do sth else
    He stopped to check the tyres. (He stopped driving to see if the tyre were OK)
    He went on to explain how it should be done. (He changed the subject)
try
  • + gerund = an experiment or a new experience
    I can’t sleep. Try taking a sleeping pill.
  • + infinitive = an attempt which might succeed or fail
    He tried to open the door but couldn’t.
mean
  • + gerund = what is involved
    Getting to work on time means getting up at 6:30am.
  • + infinitive = intend
    I didn’t mean to upset you.

She is considered to be very clever. (have an opinion)

I am considering moving in England. (think about)

I need to do it.

It needs to be done.

It needs doing.

They required him to get a driving licence. (asked from him)

These files require sorting out. (need)

hear, see, watch

  • + infinitive without to = action complete
    I saw John lift the weight. (I observed the whole action)
  • + gerund = action in progress
    I saw Mary typing the letters. (I don’t know if or when she finished the action)

prefer doing sth to doing sth else

I prefer skiing to climbing.
